The honest answer

The MOU is not obviously a bad deal.

It would bring major new revenue to Newfoundland & Labrador and finally move past the 1969 arrangement that many believe cost the province billions over five decades. That much is real, and worth acknowledging.

But the documents also reveal serious unanswered questions about pricing escalation, transmission access, long-term flexibility, and what happens after 2050. Questions that should ideally be settled before a final binding agreement is signed.

The strongest position is neither "sign now" nor "walk away." It is to strengthen the weak terms, then sign a better deal.

The main risk

Do not repeat the mistake.

The 1969 deal was not viewed as unfair solely because of price. It locked Newfoundland & Labrador into rigid terms for generations, with no mechanism to share in rising electricity values as decades passed.

Any new deal must avoid the same trap. A better headline price is not enough if the province loses too much flexibility, or if the escalation formula fails to track real market value over fifty years.

What the MOU gets right

Real wins worth preserving.

Money before 2041, if signed

A path to revenue years ahead of schedule.

The 1969 contract does not expire until 2041. The original MOU proposed payments retroactive to January 1, 2025, averaging roughly $1 billion per year through 2041 to the provincial treasury, compared to less than $20 million annually today. With the April 30, 2026 framework deadline passed, the retroactive trigger is no longer guaranteed and would need to be re-negotiated. Until any binding deal is signed, NL continues to receive 0.2¢/kWh under the old contract.

Innu inclusion

Indigenous partnership built in.

The Innu Nation is included as a signatory in a way the original 1969 development failed to do. Hiring priority for construction goes to Innu workers first, then Labrador residents, then the rest of the province.

Power for Labrador, if signed

A major expansion of industrial power capacity in Labrador.

If a binding agreement is reached, NL Hydro's allocation would expand significantly, from current levels to 1,990 MW carved out for industrial development in Labrador. NL would hold 60% ownership of the new Gull Island entity, with construction jobs prioritizing Innu workers and Labrador residents. Hydro-Québec would contribute approximately $3.5 billion toward NL's participation in the project. That money is tied to long-term power purchase rights, not a no-strings transfer.

Critical issue №1, Pricing

The $115 billion question.

Schedule F problem

The headline number depends on an assumption that isn't binding yet.

The widely cited "$225 billion to the provincial treasury" rests on Schedule G's forecast payment schedule, which, based on publicly available assumptions, appears to imply long-term escalation averaging roughly 5-6% annually over fifty years. The actual binding commitment in the MOU is the present value: $33.8 billion in today's dollars. How that present value translates into future payments depends on Schedule F, the escalation formula, which has not yet been negotiated in binding form.

Some independent analyses have suggested that if escalation tracks closer to 3% annually, total payments could fall substantially below the headline projections over the full term of the agreement. Based on publicly available assumptions and estimates, the starting point in 2024 dollars works out to about 2.5¢/kWh on average, only around 41% of recent New England wholesale prices.

  1. The final agreement must define the escalation formula in plain language, not leave it to future negotiation.
  2. The formula must reference verifiable benchmarks: Quebec wholesale prices, replacement cost of green energy, and Northeast export markets.
  3. The pricing floor and any deflation protection must be explicit. Energy prices have not risen consistently for the last fifteen years.
  4. The province must publish the assumptions behind the $225 billion figure before any final deal is signed.
Without a clearly defined escalation formula, the advertised long-term value of this deal is an assumption, not a guarantee.
Critical issue №2, Transmission

Who controls the wires?

Market access

Churchill power is only as valuable as the ability to move it.

Hydro-Québec controls the primary transmission corridor south to high-value markets in New York and New England. The Labrador-Island Link cannot carry the volumes needed to recapture Churchill power at scale. NL would still depend heavily on Quebec transmission access to reach many outside markets, regardless of the price structure on paper.

  1. Firm wheeling rights for Newfoundland & Labrador power through Hydro-Québec's grid must be defined in the binding agreement.
  2. Wheeling pricing and dispute resolution must be transparent and locked in, not subject to future tariff renegotiation.
  3. The province must preserve practical access to Northeast and Maritime markets via existing and future transmission.
  4. Future transmission expansion rights must be protected for the full term of the agreement.
Without firm transmission guarantees, Newfoundland & Labrador could remain heavily dependent on Quebec-controlled export access for decades.
Other concerns to address

Loose ends that matter.

Concern

Long-term lock-in

The deal extends toward 2075. Off-ramps in 2051 and 2061 are mentioned in the Fact Sheet but the underlying MOU language is less explicit. Off-ramps need to be confirmed in the final deal and brought earlier.

Concern

Pricing clarity for the public

The public deserves a plain-language explanation of nominal dollars, present value, the discount rate, and the real average price, not press releases that mix three different framings.

Concern

Most export value still flows through Quebec

Hydro-Québec would secure long-term access to a substantial portion of existing and future Churchill power through long-term purchase agreements, while NL Hydro would retain a significant allocation for Labrador industrial development. Before locking up the rest, the province should test demand from emerging users such as minerals processing, hydrogen, food production, manufacturing, and digital infrastructure, that could create jobs here rather than send electrons to Quebec.

Concern

Cost overrun protection

Hydro-Québec appears to lead on most new construction, while upgrades to existing Churchill Falls units appear to fall to NL Hydro. Cost-overrun risk for NL Hydro's portions should be explicitly capped in the final deal.

What the final deal must include

Five conditions before any signature.

  • Money in hand, not on paper.

    The original MOU promised revenue retroactive to January 1, 2025, but the framework deadline expired on April 30, 2026 without a binding agreement. Any new deal must restore the retroactive trigger, or NL loses the value of every month spent at 0.2¢/kWh while negotiations drag on.

  • A binding pricing formula.

    The escalation mechanism in any final agreement must be explicit, plain-language, and tied to verifiable benchmarks, not left to future negotiation under Schedule F principles. Without this, the advertised long-term value is a forecast, not a commitment.

  • Review windows for non-price terms.

    Even a strong pricing formula cannot capture structural shifts, including new industrial demand, transmission alternatives, ownership questions, or technologies that don't yet exist. At least one binding re-opener before 2051 to renegotiate non-price terms when the world changes.

  • Transmission and market access.

    Firm wheeling rights through Quebec, transparent tariffs, and dispute resolution, locked in, not left to future negotiation.

  • Independent public review of the final deal.

    The binding agreement, not just the MOU, must be reviewed publicly before being locked in for fifty years.

Reality check, the clock

Time is not unlimited.

Political timeline

"Fix it" only works if there is still time to fix it.

Quebec holds a provincial election on October 5, 2026. Political uncertainty could affect the future of negotiations, especially if a new government takes a different position on the MOU.

The original April 30, 2026 target for binding agreements has passed. The framework now sits in limbo, with talks reduced to the minimum needed to keep things alive. Premier Wakeham has restated his commitment to a public referendum on any final deal.

The negotiating window remains tight. Quebec's October 2026 election adds urgency. Failure to reach a revised deal soon could risk the current terms entirely if a government opposed to the current framework takes office.

Beyond the contract

Use the power to build the economy here.

The MOU is about more than selling electrons to Quebec. It is about whether Newfoundland & Labrador uses its remaining hydroelectric potential to generate cash flow on paper or to anchor industries that create real jobs in the province.

Before locking up future capacity, the province should test real demand from industries that need clean, firm power.

What winning looks like

What NL needs to actually win.

"Fix it, then sign" is not a slogan. It is a list of specific asks the binding agreement must contain. Anything less, and the province has spent fifty years of leverage for a better-but-not-good-enough version of the same trade.

  • An industrial recruitment guarantee.

    Reserve 500 to 1,000 MW of new capacity at competitive rates for in-province industrial users, including hydrogen production, AI and data centres, advanced manufacturing, and mineral processing. If NL cannot develop demand within ten years, Quebec gets first refusal at the same rate. Without this, the 1,990 MW Labrador allocation risks becoming stranded capacity Quebec eventually buys at a discount.

  • Transparent revenue accounting.

    The government must publish exactly how much Churchill Falls revenue is discretionary versus already committed to Muskrat Falls rate mitigation, debt servicing, and existing obligations. The public deserves to know whether this deal funds schools and hospitals, or just covers existing bills.

  • An escalation floor in dollars.

    Whatever Schedule F ends up saying, the binding agreement must guarantee a minimum total present value, not a forecast that can swing by $115 billion based on which assumption proves correct. The floor should be expressed in dollars, indexed to inflation, with no escape clauses.

  • Transmission rights with teeth.

    Binding wheeling rights to New England, New York, and Ontario at cost-plus pricing, not whatever Hydro-Québec decides to charge. Independent arbitration for disputes. Future expansion rights protected for the full term. Without this, every other concession is worth less than it looks.

  • Cost overrun clarity, project by project.

    Hydro-Québec appears to carry the lead risk on Gull Island, while NL Hydro appears responsible for Churchill Falls upgrades and Labrador transmission. The final deal must spell out which entity bears risk for each line item, and not leave NL exposed to surprises during construction.

The public's leverage

Use the referendum as leverage.

Premier Wakeham has committed to putting any final deal to a public referendum before signing. That commitment is the single most powerful tool the public has, and it should be used deliberately.

Any binding agreement that fails to meet the conditions outlined in this brief should fail that referendum. The government should know that going in. So should Quebec.

This brief is not a campaign for or against a deal. It is a roadmap for informed voting when the time comes. The independent review committee's report on May 19 will add detail. The final agreement, if and when it appears, will need the same scrutiny.

The 1969 deal passed with almost no public scrutiny. That is the mistake worth not repeating.
